Diet and exercise

Nutrition and physical activity interventions in preschool and child care offer young children opportunities to eat healthy foods and engage in physical activity throughout the day. Nutrition interventions provide fruits, vegetables, and other healthy foods as part of snacks, meals, taste-testing, and food preparation, frequently with basic nutrition education. Physical activity interventions provide opportunities for children to increase their physical activity, typically by training teachers to incorporate physical activity into daily routines, changing the physical environment, or offering more time for physical activity. Government regulations and state and organizational policies can support these interventions through guidelines for healthy food consumption, physical activity, and screen time, and support for playground improvements1, 2, 3, with additional support to help providers reach compliance4.

What could this strategy improve?

Expected Benefits

Our evidence rating is based on the likelihood of achieving these outcomes:

  • Improved nutrition

  • Increased physical activity

  • Improved weight status

Potential Benefits

Our evidence rating is not based on these outcomes, but these benefits may also be possible:

  • Increased fruit & vegetable consumption

  • Improved physical fitness

What does the research say about effectiveness?

There is strong evidence that nutrition interventions in preschool and child care improve childrenâ€™s diets5, 6, 7, 8, 9 and physical activity interventions increase their activity levels5, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14. Such interventions may improve childrenâ€™s weight outcomes15, 16, 17 and obesity-related behaviors such as screen use5. However, variation in the design, duration, and implementation of these interventions can result in varied effects18, 19. Additional evidence is needed to determine if these interventions also reduce risk of overweight or obesity14, 18, 20.

Adhering to nutrition guidelines in preschool and child care can decrease childrenâ€™s fat intake and increase fruit and vegetable intake6, 20. Implementing physical activity interventions can increase participantsâ€™ fitness and motor skills14. In some circumstances, nutrition and physical activity interventions have been shown to reduce childrenâ€™s weight, body fat, or body mass index (BMI)6, 15, 16, 17, 18, 21. Interventions that include strong parent engagement components are more effective at improving weight outcomes15, 16, 17, increasing physical activity5, 11 and improving nutrition5 than interventions that do not engage parents. Multi-component, multi-level, comprehensive interventions are more effective in improving participantsâ€™ weight status than less comprehensive interventions, however, such interventions can be challenging to implement15.

Interventions that increase time for physical activity, provide portable play equipment (e.g., balls and other objects), and include playground markings have been shown to increase moderate to vigorous physical activity13, 22, 23. Adding portable play equipment has greater effects on physical activity than adding fixed play structures and equipment, since children tend to gather and stand still on or under fixed structures13. An intervention at rural Head Start programs that promoted specific activities (ball playing, dancing, active games, jumping, and walking) improved participating preschool childrenâ€™s physical activity at home, including among Native American and Hispanic children24. In Massachusetts, state early education standards include a multi-component nutrition and physical activity intervention that is associated with increased childrenâ€™s physical activity1.

Interventions with adult-led physical activity, and which have children go outside regardless of weather, are more likely to meet best practice standards for physical activity and outdoor time25. Incorporating lots of taste testing opportunities into interventions can increase vegetable consumption among 2-5 year olds26 and removing TVs from child care centers reduces childrenâ€™s screen time5. Interventions that include structured activity, strong parent engagement components, expert facilitators, a grounding in theory11, and environmental as well as child engagement components5 may be more effective than those that do not. Researchers recommend that child care centers train teachers in how to integrate physical activity into learning to maximize effects13, 14, 19. In general, staff training increases effectiveness of interventions5 and teacher-led interventions may be more cost effective16. The cost of healthy food and inclement weather are frequent challenges to meeting best practice standards for nutrition and physical activity interventions27.

Some experts suggest more intensive parent engagement strategies can improve intervention effectiveness11, for example, behavioral therapy, physical activity training, education lessons, group meetings, and physical activity and education sessions with their children16. Consistent educational materials across preschool or care settings and homes improves effectiveness of interventions17. Experts recommend interventions last longer than six months and include tailoring for community needs and consideration of cultural contexts; without tailoring, interventions may be more effective among more advantaged socio-economic groups10. Culturally sensitive interventions are implemented with a clear understanding of cultural values, generally with the help of bilingual and bicultural facilitators, and often include translated and literacy-appropriate materials, social support, and family-based activities28.

How could this strategy impact health disparities? This strategy is rated no impact on disparities likely.
Implementation Examples

The Public Health Law Centerâ€™s (PHLC) 50-state review of early childhood care and education regulations includes nutrition and physical activity standards; the on-line map is searchable by state29. Terms and standards relating to nutrition, physical activity, and screen time vary widely across the country; however, very few states incorporate their standards into their statewide regulations30. The National Resource Center for Health and Safety in Child Care and Early Education (NRC) identifies 47 potential components for licensing standards that support healthy dietary behaviors and physical activity for young children3. As of 2018, Tennessee, North Carolina, and Nevadaâ€™s child care licensing regulations are the most supportive of healthy weight practices based on these 47 components31.

The national program Healthy Kids, Healthy Future (HKHF) offers childhood obesity prevention resources and tools in English and Spanish to assist child care and preschool providers, state and local leaders, and trainers32. HKHF also supports the Early Care and Education Learning Collaborative (ECELC) in Alabama, Arizona, Florida, Indiana, Kansas, Kentucky, California, Missouri, New Jersey, and Virginia to introduce policy and program changes to improve healthy eating, breastfeeding supports, physical activity, and screen time in early child care and education settings33. Illinois adopted a resolution in 2012 calling for daily, quality physical education in preschool and early child care centers, among other child obesity prevention measures34.

In some states, nutrition and physical activity interventions focus on developing curriculum and training early childhood care and education providers through non-profit and public sector partnerships, as in Idaho35. The Minnesota Department of Health supports a two-step, train-the-trainer approach which includes follow-up technical assistance to early care and education providers implementing best practices36. Cross-sector partnerships in early child care and education also exist in Hawaii37, North Dakota38, and Alaska, whose Department of Health resources include Tundra to Table nutrition videos for early childhood providers and families39.

The national Racial and Ethnic Approaches to Community Health (REACH) program offers grants to develop and support local, culturally appropriate programs to address health issues; grants can be used to fund activities in early childhood settings40. Free resources, such as self-assessment checklists for providers in Spanish and English, are available through the National Center for Cultural Competence41.
